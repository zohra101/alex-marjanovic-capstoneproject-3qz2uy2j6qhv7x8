About me

A writer since third grade, during high school (in New Jersey), my dad wanted to buy me a really big present. "Do you want a computer like your cousins?" And so began my war between art and computing. I graduated with both journalism and programming classes, and served as the co-editor-in-chief of both the school paper and yearbook (thankfully, not in the same year!). At Rutgers, I dropped computer science because I had more fun around books, old things, and people; I majored in English and Italian language and literature, with a minor in Mediaeval Studies focused on European history.

What can you do with an English degree? Almost anything! After selling computers and office machines at Sears, I joined inbound sales and then technical supoort at a call center, until the company went out of business. On a sales force automation help desk, supporting CRM software and equipment for biotech and pharma sales representatives, I trainied new techs and wrote a lot of SOPs. Working directly with both clients and technical staff gave me unparalleled insight into the needs of users of all levels of backgrpund, experience, and goals.

Have you tried explaining that after being on the phone all day, talking on the phone is the last thing you want to do? Knowing an advanced degree would help my career, I researched programs in communications, creative writing, and Italian, and came across technical writing. Realizing this field would combine my loves for technology and written words, plus allow more energy for people outside work, I enrolled in the fantastic certificate program at the New Jersey Institute of Technology, and rolled right into the Master's.

Computer-based training, knowledge management, and user-centered design research completed, I relocated to Baton Rouge, LA, to become a business intelligence developer. (You read that correctly, not a technica writer.) I always liked tinkering with databases, and an opportunity to move out of the cold weather? Yes! When our team moved into the Application Development umbrella, developers and managers alike were excited to acquire a technical writer to take over documenting the software they produced.
